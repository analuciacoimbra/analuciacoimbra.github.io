Beyond the Shopping List: Understanding Grocery Preparation Habits and Behaviours

Context:

In 2024/25, after a period of self-study to learn new skills and responsibilities (focusing mainly on UX Research and related topics), I decided to volunteer my time and expertise. That way, I would be able to consolidate my knowledge and gain more practical experience.

During that time, I found a freelance project on social media about grocery shopping and price comparison that I found very interesting. As such, I contacted those responsible for the aforementioned project (hereinafter referred to as stakeholders) to offer my services as a volunteer UX Researcher. My only requirement was to be able to use this experience in my portfolio as a case study whilst keeping the most sensitive information confidential, which was promptly accepted.

The problem

  • The project originated from the stakeholders' needs. They wanted to build a tool that helped them analyse if the current price for a product was the cheapest they could pay or if they could find the item cheaper somewhere else.
  • There are many supermarkets and promotions happening all the time, which makes it harder to track the base price for the products for each one of them.
  • From the stakeholders’ perspective, some of the promotions seemed to be misleading: as in, the stores would raise the effective current price of the item so they could offer the base price as a promotion. These tactics are hard to notice unless they have data from previous time frames to compare. This prompted the creation of this tool.
  • With the rise of global inflation in the post-COVID-19 era, the stakeholders recognised the potential utility of this project for the general public, whilst lacking any concrete information or research about the needs of this potential target audience.

Handwritten grocery planning materials showing price comparisons across different supermarkets and grocery lists written in post-its.

After a meeting with the stakeholders, I proposed advancing with generative research to understand user needs and shopping behaviours, rather than immediately proceeding with evaluative research on an existing solution, in order to ensure alignment between the project and user needs and to inform future project decisions.

Preparation

Business/project goals

  • Stakeholders were interviewed regarding project goals and research expectations. Across the board, the stakeholders’ main ambition for this project is to positively impact people by providing a tool that allows them to effectively save money and access better deals.
  • To systematically identify research needs, I facilitated a Knowledge Quadrant exercise, as proposed by Debbie Levitt. We used a shared Miro board during our meeting and finalised it asynchronously by an agreed deadline.
  • A preliminary analysis of the research needs was performed and the questions were tagged accordingly (questions that UX Research could answer and questions that should be answered by data analytics). The data analytics questions were considered out of scope for this generative research study.
  • The completed quadrant served as the foundation for our research questions and helped maintain stakeholder alignment throughout the investigation.

Existing Research/Software/Process

  • There was no previous research and no information about current software or processes used by the possible target audience, which further emphasised the need to explore these areas in our study.

Secondary Research

  • Before moving forward with planning, I conducted targeted secondary research to:
    • Identify existing information that might answer some of our research questions
    • Establish baseline understanding about potential target audiences
    • Define potential guidelines for the primary research
  • Key findings included:
    • Grocery shopping preparation is predominantly done by women (9 out of 10 people), who also typically do the shopping afterwards.
    • During this inflationary period, 6 out of 10 Portuguese consumers were using grocery shopping lists, higher than in previous years, primarily to control expenses and reduce impulse purchases.
    • Media coverage showed that despite government initiatives like "IVA Zero" (Zero VAT) for essential items, prices continued to rise.
    • People were adapting by reducing non-essential spending, exploring alternative shopping venues like local markets, and making more frequent but smaller shopping trips.
    • Several potential competing tools were identified, highlighting the need for competitive analysis.
  • With these findings, it’s possible to conclude that there could be market demand for the proposed tool, while also identifying potential competitors that would require further investigation.
  • This information was compiled in a table on Miro with the main takeaways of each article/study and respective sources to be consulted as needed.

Main research questions

  • How do people choose which supermarket to go to?
  • Why do people do this preparation?
  • Which stores/products do people research the most before buying?
  • Is there a difference in the approach to discounts/promotions by different age groups?
  • Do people go to one single supermarket or do they go to several?
  • Do people set a budget before going shopping?
  • Do they use any apps/websites or other tools?
  • How do people decide if it's a good discount or not?
  • What are their pain points?
  • Can people spot which products are labelled for allergens or other specific needs?
  • What do people write on their shopping lists?
  • What information do people need to know about their products?
  • How do people decide what to buy?
  • Do people tend to be loyal to a brand/store?
  • What criteria does a person use to choose between a brand and a white label product?
  • What kind of products do people prefer to buy at a discount?
  • Do people only purchase what's on their shopping list or do they end up buying more products?
  • Do people validate the prices on the shopping list to confirm that they match the preparation they've made?
  • Do people check the price of products before, during or after shopping?

Planning

The following research methods were selected to address the research questions:

  • Selected as the primary methodology to gather user data due to time and location constraints. Interviews needed to be conducted within project timeframes while capturing input from a diverse pool of participants across Portugal, representing various age groups, genders, and including people with permanent and temporary impairments.
  • Whenever possible and necessary, observation was also used by prompting the interviewees to show their current processes and apps they use, including how they use them.
  • Contextual inquiry for each of the sub-tasks would have been the most adequate method to observe people in their own settings and contexts whilst performing the task. The users' performance may vary according to the setting (for example, doing the task in a controlled environment versus their own environment) and that is something that may add a new layer of complexity and detail. However, as this was a volunteer position with no budget available and significant time constraints, this approach was unfeasible despite being methodologically better.
  • Conducted research across social media platforms to gather data on user preferences, including the most preferred supermarkets and their perceived quality tier lists.
  • Deployed a survey to collect additional quantitative data on shopping habits and preferences.
  • This combined data was critical for establishing implementation priorities, such as which supermarkets to add to the app first based on user preference rankings and perceived quality.
  • Preparing the shopping list and then going shopping with it, although an apparently simple task, is very complex given the task dimensions. It is, above all, very cognitively demanding as it may require knowing criteria such as family/personal preferences, previous prices and mental arithmetic.
  • As such, task analysis was conducted so that this cognitive load and knowledge dependencies could be mapped.
  • Along with the task analysis mapping, user knowledge profiles and a task optimisation mapping were included.
  • During secondary research, seemingly similar tools were found. That was the primary reason for including this methodology.
  • However, another iteration was needed when none of the users interviewed reported using the tools found during secondary research. Even though they weren't considered competition at first glance, since those were the users' choices, more analysis was required.
  • Said tools were compared to the currently existing project to find commonalities, differences and how they answer the users' needs, pains and motivations.

Recruitment

  • Participants were recruited on social media using a screener. The target audience should live in Portugal and do any sort of preparation before going shopping.
  • The screener allowed us to define criteria such as different efforts in the preparation (simple or extensive preparation) or going to one or more supermarkets, which was critical for the study considering the discovered behavioural typologies (or user knowledge profiles/personas) during the interviews.
  • From over 90 potential respondents, approximately 40 were selected to ensure diversity across age groups, locations, occupations, genders, and including people with permanent and temporary impairments.
  • It was possible to get a large and diverse pool of participants, allowing further refinement and detail in the user knowledge profiles.

Budget/Incentives

  • Incentives weren’t available since it was an unpaid study.

Alignment

  • After finishing the research preparation and planning, the team had a meeting to align expectations and approve the research plan to start as soon as possible.

Execution

User Interviews with Observations

  • Semi-structured interviews were conducted with observations of tools and workflows when relevant
  • Total of 26 sessions (24 remote, 2 in-person), each lasting 1-1.5 hours
  • Participants represented diverse demographics across age groups, locations, occupations, and genders
  • Session documentation included detailed notes capturing key answers and observations
  • All interviews were recorded with participant informed consent for comprehensive analysis
  • Dovetail was used to obtain transcripts

Social Media Research and Survey

  • Explored organic discussions on Reddit and Facebook, where users shared "supermarket tier lists", compared shopping venues and exchanged money-saving strategies
  • Created a concise Google Forms survey about shopping habits and preferences, including questions about supermarket selection and preferred promotion types, obtaining 147 completed surveys after posting on social media
  • Analysed survey responses to extract meaningful patterns and insights about shopping behaviours
  • Translated research findings into concrete recommendations for which supermarkets should be prioritised in the product development roadmap
  • Leveraged combined qualitative (social media discussions) and quantitative (survey) data with the other research data to validate and strengthen research insights

Task analysis

  • Analysis based on data gathered through user interviews and observational sessions
  • Followed a two-stage mapping process:
    • First pass: Initial high-level task flow mapping
    • Second pass: Detailed refinement of the complete task, thoroughly exploring two critical sub-tasks (grocery trip preparation and in-store shopping)
  • Incorporated the user knowledge profiles (behavioural typologies) to document knowledge variations across groups (common knowledge, required knowledge, and knowledge gaps)
  • Mapped task execution patterns for each of the user profiles to capture process variations
  • Conducted regular asynchronous stakeholder check-ins throughout task optimisation to ensure implementation feasibility
  • Visualised and managed all maps and user knowledge profiles on a Miro board

Competitive Analysis

  • Assessment of 11 grocery shopping applications and websites identified through secondary research and user interviews
  • Comparative assessment using 50+ criteria across all platforms
  • Testing conducted using Bluestacks for OS environment emulation with supplementary tools as needed
  • Documented findings through detailed notes and interface screenshots for reference

Analysis

Analysis

  • Conducted thematic analysis of interview data using Dovetail
  • Iteratively analysed interview transcripts to identify emerging patterns and themes
  • Gradually developed user knowledge profiles as patterns became apparent through sequential interviews

Refinement

  • Expanded participant pool from the initial 8 interviews to 26 total participants after recognising emerging patterns that required further investigation
  • Iterated on user knowledge profiles, ultimately identifying 3 primary profiles plus 1 additional indirect profile that emerged during the interviews and proved relevant to task execution
  • Used iterative analysis to refine task mapping and develop a comprehensive understanding of users' needs, pain points and motivations
  • Conducted competitive analysis in two phases:
    • Initial assessment based on solutions identified through secondary research
    • Supplementary assessment incorporating the tools mentioned by participants during interviews

Delivery

Summary

  • Confirming the Need: The research confirmed that preparing for grocery shopping is common practice among the Portuguese population. Secondary research had already suggested that a small majority do this, which was reinforced when 81,9% of our survey respondents reported doing some sort of preparation. Most importantly, saving money emerged as the primary motivation, mentioned by every single interviewee, which validates the core purpose of this project.
  • Gap in Current Solutions: Despite several existing tools in the market, none of them fully address the needs uncovered during this investigation—not even the ones our interviewees currently use. This forces people to spend more time preparing for grocery shopping and to settle for whatever tools they can find to reduce their pain points as much as possible.
  • Research Informing Design and Development: By conducting thorough research with different user types, I was able to uncover many important needs, frustrations, motivations and preferences. These findings now provide a solid foundation for the team to improve the existing solution, hopefully creating something that better meets people's needs and genuinely helps them save money during this challenging economic period.

Synthesis

In order to address the pains and needs related to the whole process of preparing the grocery shopping list and the inevitable shopping trip, I analysed and synthesised all the data into insights following structured approach with findings and actionable recommendations. There were insights covering 8 different main themes, including:

  • Preparing for the shopping trip
  • Task delegation
  • Decision-making
  • Cost control

As an example, I'll share one of the key insights.

  • Task delegation
    • Finding:
      • In addition to frequent shoppers and non-frequent shoppers, several interviewees mentioned that they don't always do the shopping themselves, although they always prepare the shopping trip. Some of the reasons reported were illness, recovery from an accident/childbirth and convenience (for example, their partner being closer to the shopping venue or their own unavailability). However, due to the other person's lack of experience, they have to do extra work detailing the products (for instance, instead of writing 'milk', they have to specify 'a carton of Agros semi-skimmed milk'). If they need to use vouchers, they also have to add a note.
      • It was also reported that this person is often unaware of the family's usual/preferred brands and which products can be substituted for others or which should not be purchased if they are not in stock/on promotion.
      • The usual consequences of not providing sufficient detail are constant phone calls to confirm information and the purchase of unnecessary or unsuitable products, creating frustration for both parties and extending both the time and expenses required to complete the shopping task.
    • Recommendation:
      • To reduce the pain points related to the delegation of the shopping task, the following improvements are suggested:
        • Contextual Notes: Enable the possibility of adding notes to the shopping list (for example, to note down possible vouchers to use)
        • Product-Specific Instructions: Provide the option of adding notes per product (for example, to add information about possible replacement products or important details about the product) and saving those product notes as editable user preferences to be reused in future shopping trips
        • Collaborative Shopping: Allow sharing the shopping list with the possibility of crossing off products already placed in the basket or already purchased
        • Real-time Synchronisation: Ensure the shopping list of the "organiser" and the "shopper" remains synchronised throughout the shopping journey
        • Customisable Units: Enable customising the units to be purchased (for example, '10 slices of ham') to reduce ambiguity for the shopper

Project outcomes

Alignment (with the team)

  • The research findings were presented to stakeholders (developers) in a dedicated meeting and subsequently shared for future reference and implementation guidance.
  • The prioritisation matrix then became the central focus of the discussion. Since not all suggestions could be assessed for technical feasibility during the meeting, the matrix was shared for asynchronous completion by team members when they had the capacity to evaluate them.

Product/Design

  • Research insights and recommendations will inform the revamp of the existing product. Given the volunteer nature of this project across all roles, implementation will be gradual, with impact being measured over time.
  • The research established a robust foundation of user understanding that will guide product decisions moving forward, creating a more user-centred development approach.

Takeways

What was learned from this experience

There are several key lessons derived from this research that I would approach differently in future research:

  • Most notably, the fourth user knowledge profile warrants deeper investigation (to note: this profile refers to friends or family members who do the shopping part of the task but lack familiarity with household preferences).

    While I captured insights about their needs indirectly through interviews with list creators (who have experience with them and know how to make a list that meets their needs as much as possible), direct conversations with these proxy shoppers would have provided valuable complementary perspectives.

    These individuals require specially detailed lists precisely because they don't possess the product knowledge that list creators have. Due to project constraints, I couldn't conduct separate recruitment for this cohort. In future studies, I would implement a snowball sampling technique, asking primary participants if their shopping helpers might be available for brief interviews, creating a more holistic view of this interdependent shopping ecosystem.

  • The interview process became quite iterative in nature, which proved insightful. Initial interviews revealed distinct behavioural patterns that prompted me to strategically recruit additional participants to ensure proper representation across emerging user profiles.

    While this flexible approach was feasible within a volunteer study context, I recognise that, in a professional setting with incentive budgets and tight deadlines, such adaptation would require careful planning and stakeholder alignment. This flexibility ultimately strengthened the findings as the investigation began without a clearly defined target audience.

Challenges

There were several limitations during this project that I have reflected upon.

  • Budget/recruitment
    • Problem: As an unpaid study conducted during the summer months, recruitment proved difficult. Some email communications were flagged as spam, and many potential participants were unavailable after completing the screener due to holiday schedules.
    • Solution: I expanded the initial recruitment pool and continued sending invitations until I had at least the minimum number required for each group/typology. For future projects, I would consider seasonal timing more carefully and advocate for participant incentives to improve response rates.
  • Delays
    • Problem: Several factors extended the project beyond initial timelines: prolonged recruitment periods due to seasonal availability, expanded interview sample to accommodate emerging behavioural typologies, and unforeseen health considerations.
    • Solution: Transparent communication with stakeholders proved effective. As all stakeholders were contributing to the project alongside full-time employment, they understood the situation and accommodated necessary timeline adjustments.
  • Forgetfulness
    • Problem: Spontaneous ideas occurring outside dedicated project work time were occasionally lost due to inconsistent documentation practices.
    • Solution: I've since implemented a comprehensive note-taking system using both physical and digital formats (using Google Keep, for example) to capture insights regardless of context or location.
  • Transcriptions
    • Problem: Automated transcription services struggled with European Portuguese, requiring substantial manual corrections.
    • Solution: After testing multiple options, I selected Dovetail as the most accurate solution, though it still required some adjustments. For future non-English interviews, I'll investigate emerging AI-based transcription tools with better language support.
